# Modern Minimal Garden Style
# Use when user selects "Modern & minimal" style archetype.

---

## Philosophy

Modern minimal gardens are defined by restraint — a limited plant palette used with confidence, strong geometric structure, and an emphasis on space, form, and texture over flower abundance. The style is rooted in mid-20th century modernist landscape design (influenced by Roberto Burle Marx, Thomas Church, and later Christophe Périgon and Andy Sturgeon in the UK context).

In contemporary UK garden design, the style emphasises:
- **Edited plant palette** — often just 3–5 species used in large, bold repetitions
- **Structural hard landscaping** — corten steel, poured concrete, large-format porcelain tiles, dark timber decking
- **Architectural plants** — chosen for form, not flower; foliage and structure year-round
- **Negative space** — deliberately leaving space as a design element
- **Night lighting** — uplighting specimens is often central to the design

This style suits contemporary homes, roof terraces, courtyard gardens, and clients who want a high-impact, low-fuss outdoor space.

## Characteristic Plants

### Structural specimens (the focus plants)
- **Betula utilis** var. jacquemontii — white-stemmed birch; outstanding winter presence; use in threes for impact
- **Cercis canadensis** 'Forest Pansy' — dark purple heart-shaped leaves; spring flowers on bare stems
- **Magnolia stellata** — white starry flowers on bare stems in early spring; architectural branching structure
- **Pinus sylvestris** 'Watereri' (dwarf Scots pine) — sculptural year-round
- **Taxus baccata** (yew) — clipped into cylinders, balls, or cubes; perfect for formal minimal gardens. Note: all parts toxic.

### Grass-forward planting (modern minimal leans heavily on grasses)
- **Stipa gigantea** — golden oat grass; spectacular flowering stems to 1.8m; evergreen base; specimen use
- **Calamagrostis x acutiflora** 'Karl Foerster' — vertical, stiff, pencil-form; excellent in rows or drifts
- **Hakonechloa macra** 'Aureola' — gold-variegated, cascading; excellent in containers or shade
- **Miscanthus sinensis** 'Strictus' (porcupine grass) — banded leaves; upright; late season seed heads
- **Deschampsia cespitosa** — cloud-forming grass; shimmers in low light; shade-tolerant

### Bold perennials for repeat planting
- **Agapanthus** 'Headbourne Hybrids' (H4) — strap leaves, blue/white drumhead flowers; sculptural seedheads
- **Hemerocallis** (daylily) — if using, choose single colour, not mixed; 'Stella de Oro' (yellow) or dark reds
- **Astelia chathamica** (silver spear) — H3–H4; silver strap leaves; dramatic foliage
- **Libertia grandiflora** — grass-like, white flowers, orange-tinted seed pods; elegant minimalism
- **Kniphofia** (red hot poker) — used sparingly as punctuation; architectural flower spikes

### Evergreen foliage backbone
- **Buxus sempervirens** (box) — topiary spheres, hedges; use with box blight resilience in mind; consider alternatives
- **Ilex crenata** (Japanese holly) — box alternative; Blight-resistant; clips identically to box
- **Pittosporum tenuifolium** — H3–H4; dark stems, light leaves; use in sheltered gardens
- **Sarcococca** (sweet box) — low, evergreen, winter-fragrant; shade-tolerant; excellent for minimal gardens

## Do's and Don'ts

**Do:**
- Choose one or two architectural plants and repeat them — repetition is what makes minimal planting work
- Invest in structural hard landscaping — the planting is secondary to the bones of the design
- Use containers as sculptural elements — simple forms (cube, cylinder) in corten, concrete, or zinc
- Include year-round interest: winter bark, evergreen structure, autumn grass seedheads
- Light the garden — uplighting a specimen birch or grass planting transforms the space at night

**Don't:**
- Mix too many species — every additional species dilutes the impact
- Use cottage-style self-seeders — they undermine the controlled aesthetic
- Plant traditional herbaceous borders or mixed planting — minimal is not the same as sparse cottage
- Choose overly colourful, complex-looking flowers (dahlias, cottage roses, annuals) — they clash
- Underestimate maintenance of clipped elements — hedges and topiary need frequent cutting to look sharp

## Soil and Aspect Compatibility

**Best in:** Any well-maintained soil; improve drainage on clay with organic matter and grit for grasses. Grasses particularly dislike waterlogging in winter.

**Aspect:** Most modern minimal plants prefer full sun or partial shade. The grass-heavy palette is most spectacular in full sun. Hakonechloa is the exception — prefers shade.

**Maintenance:** Low to medium once established. Key maintenance: clipping topiary and hedging (twice yearly for box/Ilex), cutting back grasses in late winter (leave seedheads through winter), replacing any frost-damaged tender specimens.
