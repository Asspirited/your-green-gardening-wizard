## Philosophy

The cottage garden is the most quintessentially British style — abundance over precision, self-seeding over control, romance over geometry. It originated in working-class rural gardens where utility plants (vegetables, herbs) mingled freely with flowers. The Victorian and Edwardian era elevated it into an aesthetic. Gertrude Jekyll and William Robinson codified its principles in the late 19th century.

Core principle: **organised chaos**. The planting looks effortless but requires deliberate plant selection — things that self-seed, sprawl, and interweave without looking weedy. The gardener's job is curation, not control.

Key characteristics:
- Billowing, romantic abundance — no bare soil visible in summer
- Soft, informal paths (often brick or grass) flanked by overflowing borders
- Climbing plants on walls, arches, and structures — roses and clematis especially
- Strong use of sweet-smelling plants (roses, sweet peas, lavender, phlox)
- Vegetables and herbs integrated into ornamental borders
- Colour palette typically soft: pastels, white, pale yellow, soft pinks and purples. Bold hot colours used as accents only.
- Plants allowed to self-seed and move around the garden

## Characteristic Plants

### Structural backbone
- **Rosa** (old-fashioned shrub roses) — 'Gertrude Jekyll', 'Reine des Violettes', 'Fantin-Latour', 'The Generous Gardener' for climbers
- **Clematis** — through roses or on walls: C. 'Niobe', C. viticella 'Etoile Violette', C. montana for vigour
- **Delphiniums** — tall spires in blue, purple, white; classic cottage border back
- **Lupins** — bold spires; 'Band of Nobles' series in mixed pastels
- **Hollyhocks** (Alcea rosea) — classic against walls; self-seeds freely

### Mid-border
- **Foxgloves** (Digitalis purpurea) — essential biennial; plant annually for continuous display. Note: toxic to children and pets.
- **Sweet peas** (Lathyrus odoratus) — fragrant climbers; pick regularly to extend season
- **Phlox paniculata** — fragrant, midsummer, wide colour range; 'David' (white), 'Starfire' (red)
- **Geranium** (hardy geraniums) — 'Johnson's Blue', 'Rozanne' — sprawling fillers
- **Nepeta** (catmint) — soft blue-grey edging; 'Six Hills Giant' for borders
- **Salvia nemorosa** — 'Caradonna' or 'Ostfriesland'; long-flowering purple spires

### Self-seeding annuals and biennials (the soul of the cottage garden)
- **Nigella damascena** (love-in-a-mist) — blue/white flowers, decorative seed heads
- **Aquilegia** (columbine) — cross-pollinates freely, producing unpredictable pastel combinations
- **Eschscholzia** (California poppy) — orange, yellow; drought-tolerant self-seeder
- **Orlaya grandiflora** (white lace flower) — ethereal white umbels; pairs beautifully with roses
- **Ammi majus** (bishop's flower) — similar to Orlaya; very easy self-seeder
- **Verbena bonariensis** — tall, airy purple; self-seeds into paving cracks

### Fragrance layer
- **Lavandula** — 'Hidcote' or 'Munstead' for compact; 'Vera' for taller
- **Lilium regale** — white trumpet lilies; intensely fragrant; excellent in pots
- **Stocks** (Matthiola incana) — grow as annuals; night-fragrant
- **Pinks** (Dianthus) — 'Mrs Sinkins', 'Gran's Favourite' — traditional clove scent

## Do's and Don'ts

**Do:**
- Plant in drifts that overlap and interweave at the edges (not block planting)
- Leave seed heads through autumn and winter — structure, food for birds, and source of next year's self-seeders
- Include vegetables and herbs: fennel, artichoke, borage (all highly ornamental)
- Use structures: arches, obelisks, trellis — vertical interest is critical
- Mix scales: tall delphiniums behind, medium roses mid, low pinks and geraniums at front

**Don't:**
- Use grasses as a major feature — they belong to the natural/wild style
- Plant in rigid, symmetrical blocks — the charm is fluidity
- Over-mulch — self-seeding plants need bare soil to germinate
- Deadhead everything — leave some for self-seeding and wildlife
- Use modern single-colour bedding (petunias, busy Lizzies) — they break the romance

## Soil and Aspect Compatibility

**Best in:** Fertile, well-drained to moisture-retentive soil. Heavy clay can work with improvement (organic matter). Sandy soil suits lavender and pinks.

**Aspect:** Most cottage plants prefer full sun to partial shade. A few (foxgloves, aquilegias, some geraniums) will work in shadier conditions but the full palette requires reasonable light.

**Maintenance:** Medium-high. Regular deadheading (not total — leave some), staking tall plants, annual division of perennials, rose pruning. Not a low-maintenance style.
