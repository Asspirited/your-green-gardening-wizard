## Philosophy

The Mediterranean style draws from the gardens and landscapes of southern France, Italy, Spain, Greece, and coastal North Africa. It is defined by drought tolerance, sun-loving plants, fragrance, and an aesthetic that combines hard landscaping (gravel, terracotta, rendered walls) with loose, silvery-leaved planting.

In the UK context, this style is most at home in:
- South and south-east England (warmest, driest)
- Sheltered south-facing gardens and courtyard spaces
- Coastal gardens in mild zones (SW England, S Wales)
- Urban gardens with heat-trapping walls

The Mediterranean style has enormous practical appeal for UK gardeners in a warming climate — many Mediterranean plants are drought-tolerant, need minimal watering once established, and look spectacular from June to October.

Core principle: **structure, texture, and fragrance over flower abundance**. Silver leaves, terracotta pots, gravel mulch, and fragrant herbs create the atmosphere. Flowers are a bonus.

## Characteristic Plants

### Backbone shrubs
- **Lavandula** (lavender) — 'Hidcote' (compact, deep purple), 'Grosso' (large, commercial variety), L. stoechas (Spanish lavender, needs good drainage)
- **Rosmarinus** / **Rosmarinus officinalis** (rosemary) — 'Miss Jessopp's Upright' for hedging; prostrate forms over walls
- **Cistus** (rock rose) — C. x purpureus (pink), C. 'Snow Fire' (white); drought-tolerant, dislikes wet feet
- **Phlomis fruticosa** (Jerusalem sage) — yellow flowers, silver-grey leaves; architectural
- **Artemisia** — 'Powis Castle' (silver filigree foliage); exceptional foliage plant
- **Perovskia atriplicifolia** (Russian sage) — blue-purple spires, silver-white stems; drought-tolerant

### Architectural plants
- **Agave** (in pots — bring in for winter below -5°C) — dramatic rosette forms
- **Yucca filamentosa** — H5 hardy, stays outdoors; cream flower spikes in summer
- **Phormium** (New Zealand flax) — architectural; needs H3–H4 protection in cold regions
- **Olea europaea** (olive tree) — H4, needs well-drained soil; grow as specimen or standard in pot
- **Euphorbia characias subsp. wulfenii** — lime-green architectural flowers; evergreen; H4

### Gravel garden plants
- **Stipa tenuissima** (feather grass) — soft, golden, sways in breeze; pairs with Mediterranean plants
- **Allium** — A. hollandicum 'Purple Sensation', A. cristophii (star-shaped); self-seeds in gravel
- **Eryngium** (sea holly) — E. giganteum 'Miss Willmott's Ghost' (silver-white), E. x zabelii 'Big Blue'
- **Verbascum** (mullein) — tall yellow spires from silvery rosettes; self-seeds in gravel
- **Salvia** — S. nemorosa, S. x sylvestris, and tender S. patens (cobalt blue — lift for winter)

### Ground cover and edging
- **Thymus** (thyme) — T. serpyllum for paving cracks; T. x citriodorus for fragrance
- **Origanum laevigatum** 'Herrenhausen' — ornamental oregano; masses of tiny pink flowers
- **Sedum** / **Hylotelephium** — H. 'Herbstfreude' ('Autumn Joy'); succulent, drought-tolerant
- **Festuca glauca** (blue fescue) — steely blue-grey tufted grass; drought-tolerant

### Climbers for walls
- **Wisteria sinensis** — classic Mediterranean pergola plant; needs a south or west wall
- **Solanum crispum** 'Glasnevin' — scrambling potato vine; blue-purple flowers; H4
- **Rosa banksiae** 'Lutea' — thornless, pale yellow; needs a very warm sheltered wall; H3

## Do's and Don'ts

**Do:**
- Use gravel mulch 5–8cm deep instead of bark — it drains, dries, and looks authentic; reflects heat
- Create hard landscaping: terracotta pots, rendered or stone walls, paving with planting gaps
- Group plants in loose, uneven drifts with some intentional spacing (not packed tight)
- Use large terracotta or ceramic pots as focal points for tender species (agave, lemon trees)
- Incorporate a water feature if possible — bubbling courtyard fountain fits the style

**Don't:**
- Plant in heavy, poorly-drained clay without significant improvement — most Mediterranean plants will rot
- Choose H3 or tender plants for exposed, cold, or north-facing sites
- Use tropical-looking plants (banana, cannas, Gunnera) — they clash with the style
- Over-water once established — Mediterranean plants prefer dry summers
- Plant where frost hollows form — cold air pooling is death for these species

## Soil and Aspect Compatibility

**Best in:** Sandy, loamy, or stony, free-draining soil. Chalk works well. Heavy clay requires raised beds or significant amendment with grit and organic matter.

**Aspect:** South-facing or west-facing essential for the full palette. East-facing gardens can use the hardier Mediterranean plants (lavender, rosemary). North-facing is too cold and dark — this style will not work without compromise.

**Maintenance:** Low once established. Annual pruning of lavender (never cut into old wood), rosemary shaping, Cistus light trim. Container plants need regular feeding and watering in summer. Gravel mulch suppresses weeds effectively.
