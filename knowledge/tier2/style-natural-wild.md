## The philosophy

The Natural/Wild garden embraces the beauty of how plants grow in nature —
in drifts, communities, and self-organising combinations. It is NOT an
untidy garden. It requires more knowledge than a traditional garden to
do well, because it asks the gardener to think ecologically rather than
in terms of individual specimens.

**The key designers:**
- **Piet Oudolf** (Hauser & Wirth, High Line, Lurie Garden) — the definitive
  modern master. His "New Perennial Movement" uses grasses and long-season
  perennials for structure that persists into winter.
- **Beth Chatto** (The Gravel Garden, Essex) — "right plant, right place";
  drought-tolerant naturalistic planting; plants that thrive with minimal
  intervention.
- **Noel Kingsbury** — author and naturalistic planting theorist;
  emphasises ecological fitness over aesthetic convention.

**The reference garden:** Beth Chatto Gardens, Elmstead Market, Essex.
Also: RHS Wisley — the Prairie beds.

---

## What this style IS

- Plants in drifts and communities, not individual specimens
- Grasses as structural backbone — movement in wind is a feature
- Perennials chosen for seed heads and structure in winter, not just flowers
- Self-seeding encouraged (edit rather than control)
- High ecological value — diverse insect and bird habitat
- Four seasons of interest from the same planting
- The "mess" is intentional and beautiful — this requires confidence to maintain
- Low to medium input once established

## What this style is NOT

- It is NOT an unmaintained or neglected garden
- It is NOT about planting native-only (though natives are prioritised)
- It is NOT a wildflower meadow (that is a related but distinct style)
- It is NOT appropriate for small formal spaces, clipped hedges, or knot gardens
- It does NOT suit people who feel uncomfortable with apparent disorder

---

## Maintenance level: Low to Medium

Once established: mainly cut back in February (before new growth), divide
overgrown clumps every 3-4 years, edit self-seeders annually.
Avoid mulching with bark — leaves self-seeding to chance.

## Characteristic plants — Tier 2 quality (15+ varieties)

### Structural grasses (the backbone)
- **Calamagrostis x acutiflora 'Karl Foerster'** — upright, architectural;
  fluffy plumes June; persists into winter; H: 1.5m. Ideal Oudolf grass.
- **Panicum virgatum 'Shenandoah'** — turns red in autumn; upright habit;
  H: 1m. Stunning autumn-winter interest.
- **Molinia caerulea 'Poul Petersen'** — translucent purple-brown seed
  heads; moves in slightest breeze; H: 1.2m. Perfect with sun behind it.
- **Stipa gigantea (Golden oats)** — evergreen; enormous golden seed heads;
  H: 2m including flower. Iconic naturalistic grass.
- **Deschampsia cespitosa 'Goldtau'** — fine texture; golden summer seed
  heads; excellent in part shade; H: 60cm.
- **Pennisetum alopecuroides 'Hameln'** — fluffy bottlebrush heads; buff-pink
  autumn colour; H: 60cm. Less hardy — RHS H4, mild areas only.

### Long-season perennials
- **Echinacea purpurea 'Magnus'** — structural seed heads beloved by
  goldfinches; flowers July–September; H: 90cm. Seed heads last all winter.
- **Rudbeckia fulgida 'Goldsturm'** — intense yellow daisies; remarkably
  persistent seed heads; H: 60cm. Reliable and long-lived.
- **Persicaria amplexicaulis 'Firetail'** — outstanding long season (June–Oct);
  red bottlebrush spikes; very tough; H: 1.2m.
- **Sanguisorba officinalis** — dark wine-red bobble flowers; architectural
  stems; H: 1m. Self-seeds modestly.
- **Veronicastrum virginicum 'Fascination'** — vertical spires of pale
  lavender; H: 1.5m. Structural into winter.
- **Thalictrum delavayi 'Hewitt's Double'** — cloud of tiny lilac flowers;
  beautiful among grasses; H: 1.2m.
- **Actaea (Cimicifuga) simplex 'Brunette'** — late September spires; dark
  foliage; wonderful autumn accent; H: 1.2m.
- **Aster (Symphyotrichum) 'Little Carlow'** — mass of blue-mauve daisies
  October–November; H: 1m. Reliable for autumn colour.
- **Knautia macedonica** — scabiosa-like claret flowers; long season;
  self-seeds prolifically (edit ruthlessly or embrace); H: 60cm.

### Supporting cast
- **Allium sphaerocephalon** — egg-shaped claret heads; grows through
  grasses beautifully; July; H: 60cm.
- **Verbena bonariensis** — transparent; grows through everything; lasts
  until first frost; self-seeds; H: 1.5m.
- **Achillea 'Terracotta'** — flat heads age from orange to buff; excellent
  with grasses; H: 80cm.

## Design principles for this style

1. **Plant in drifts, not dots** — minimum 3, ideally 5-7 of each variety.
   The drift weaves through the border; no plant stands alone.
2. **Layer heights** — tall verticals (Veronicastrum) behind mid-height
   mounds (Echinacea) in front of ground-layer (Geranium, Nepeta).
3. **Think in four seasons** — spring (bulbs and emerging foliage), summer
   (flower peak), autumn (berries, seed heads, grass colour), winter (skeleton
   planting that stands through frost).
4. **Leave standing for winter** — seed heads are the point. Cut back
   only in February, just before new growth.
5. **Ratio: 60% grasses/structural plants, 40% flowering perennials** — the
   Oudolf ratio that creates that specific "meadow meets garden" aesthetic.

---

## Colour palette
- Warm purples, lavenders, and russet-amber from grasses
- Avoiding sharp acid colours or bright pinks — the palette is intentionally
  muted and naturalistic
- Orange and red used as accent only (Echinacea, Rudbeckia, Kniphofia)
- The real "colour" is textural — the way grasses catch light
