## Purpose
Plants with invasive tendencies in UK gardens — either legally controlled,
ecologically problematic, or simply garden bullies that cause regret.
Used to flag recommendations when invasive-warning extra is selected,
and to inform whether certain plants should be avoided outright.

## Prompt injection note
Injected by `buildExtrasClause(['invasive-warning'])`.
When active, any recommended plant from the "flag if recommended" list
must be explicitly flagged with management advice.

## Legally controlled species — NEVER recommend in gardens

It is an offence under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 (Schedule 9)
and the Infrastructure Act 2015 to plant or cause these to grow in the wild.
They should never be recommended and their presence in a garden should
prompt advice to report and remove properly.

- **Fallopia japonica (Japanese Knotweed)** — can grow through tarmac and
  concrete; mortgage lender issues; specialist removal required; report to
  council. NEVER recommend, always flag if user mentions it.
- **Impatiens glandulifera (Himalayan Balsam)** — river banks and damp
  areas; crowds out native plants; explosive seed dispersal.
- **Rhododendron ponticum** — woodland and acid soils; shades out native
  understorey; extremely difficult to remove. (Note: many garden Rhododendron
  hybrids are fine — it is R. ponticum specifically that is invasive.)
- **Heracleum mantegazzianum (Giant Hogweed)** — severe phototoxic sap;
  report to council if found; professional removal.
- **Carpobrotus edulis (Hottentot Fig)** — coastal areas; smothers native vegetation.

## Commonly sold but garden-invasive — flag with management advice

These are widely available and recommended in gardening media but cause
problems in many gardens. Always flag with specific containment advice.

### Buddleja davidii (Butterfly Bush)
Excellent pollinator plant but spreads prolifically by wind-blown seed.
Self-seeds into walls, railway tracks, and waste ground where it
outcompetes native plants.
**Management:** Deadhead religiously before seed sets; remove all seed heads.
Consider sterile cultivars: 'Buzz' series (compact, low fertility),
Buddleja x weyeriana sterile hybrids. Or substitute native alternatives
that are also excellent for butterflies: Verbena bonariensis (less
invasive), Lavandula, Salvia.

### Running bamboos (most Phyllostachys, Pleioblastus, Pseudosasa species)
Running bamboos spread aggressively via underground rhizomes. Can travel
several metres per year. Extremely difficult to remove once established.
Almost impossible to contain without proper barriers.
**Management:** ONLY recommend with explicit container or rhizome barrier
advice. Install a rhizome barrier (at least 60cm deep HDPE) before planting.
Clump-forming bamboos (Fargesia, Thamnocalamus) are far safer — always
recommend these over running species.

### Lysimachia punctata (Yellow Loosestrife)
Sold in garden centres but spreads vigorously underground.
**Management:** Plant in bottomless bucket sunk in ground to contain roots.

### Persicaria spp. (Knotweeds, Fleeces)
Many Persicaria are well-behaved (P. amplexicaulis, P. bistorta are
excellent garden plants). But P. campanulata and some others spread
aggressively. Check species carefully before recommending.
**Safe species:** P. amplexicaulis 'Firetail', P. bistorta 'Superba'
**Invasive tendency:** P. campanulata

### Crocosmia 'Lucifer' and other Crocosmia
Spreads rapidly by corm division in favourable conditions. Can take over
a border. Beautiful but benefits from division every 3 years.
**Management:** Divide clumps every 2-3 years and replant selected corms.

### Lythrum salicaria (Purple Loosestrife)
Fine in UK gardens but invasive in North American wetlands. Mention only
if user is in North America — irrelevant in UK context.

### Alchemilla mollis (Lady's Mantle)
Excellent garden plant but self-seeds prolifically. Not a serious problem
but worth flagging — deadhead after flowering to prevent excessive spread.

### Vinca minor and V. major (Periwinkle)
Useful ground cover for difficult dry shade, but spreads by stolons
and can smother smaller plants. Use deliberately as ground cover,
not as understorey in mixed plantings.

## Vigorous plants that need space — flag but recommend freely

These are excellent garden plants but need room or management. Not invasive
in the ecological sense but can overwhelm smaller neighbours if not planned for.

- **Macleaya cordata (Plume Poppy)** — magnificent but spreads by root suckers;
  needs space or containment
- **Melianthus major** — in mild areas can become huge; may self-seed
- **Acanthus mollis (Bear's Breeches)** — extraordinary deep roots once
  established; site carefully as very hard to move
- **Eupatorium cannabinum** — tall, magnificent for wildlife but spreads;
  deadhead if spread unwanted
- **Erigeron karvinskianus (Mexican Fleabane)** — charming self-seeder in walls
  and paving gaps; may need managing
- **Centranthus ruber (Red Valerian)** — wonderful but prolific self-seeder
  in free-draining soils

## Better alternatives to common invasives

| Invasive/problematic | Better UK-native or non-invasive alternative |
|---|---|
| Buddleja davidii | Verbena bonariensis, Lavandula, Salvia nemorosa |
| Running bamboo | Fargesia robusta, F. murieliae (clumping bamboos) |
| Lysimachia punctata | Lysimachia clethroides (clump-forming, less invasive) |
| Rhododendron ponticum | Named Rhododendron hybrids, Kalmia latifolia |
| Fallopia japonica | If present: get specialist removal, not a planting alternative |
