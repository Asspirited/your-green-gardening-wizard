# UK Seasonal Gardening Calendar
# Feeds buildSeasonalPrompt() in domain.js
# UK-specific timings — not generic Northern Hemisphere advice.

---

## Overview

UK gardening seasons do not map neatly to calendar months. Use these as guidelines,
always adjusted for the user's region (Scotland Highlands run 4–6 weeks later than SW England).

---

## Spring (March – May)

### March
**What to do:**
- Sow hardy annuals and vegetables under cover (tomatoes, aubergines, peppers)
- Plant bare-root roses and hedging — last chance before ground warms
- Divide established perennials (hostas, agapanthus, ornamental grasses)
- Prune roses (hybrid teas to 30cm, shrub roses lightly)
- Apply slow-release fertiliser to borders and containers
- Chit seed potatoes in egg boxes — 3–4 weeks before outdoor planting

**Watch for:**
- Late frosts — forecast to mid-April in most of England, May in Scotland
- Slug and snail activity picks up fast — use pellets, copper tape, or nematodes
- Viburnum beetle larvae on Viburnum tinus

**Planting out:**
- Do NOT plant tender summer bedding until risk of frost has passed (May in most regions)
- Hardy annuals can go out mid-March in SW England; wait until April elsewhere

---

### April
**What to do:**
- Plant summer-flowering bulbs: gladioli, dahlias (tubers once soil warms), lilies
- Start direct-sowing hardy vegetables outdoors: lettuce, beetroot, spinach, peas
- Mow lawns for the first time — cut high (5–6cm) for first cut
- Apply pre-emergent weed killer to gravel and paths if needed
- Pot up tender perennials wintered indoors (begonias, cannas)
- Start regular feeding of container plants

**Watch for:**
- Aphid colonies appearing on young growth — rub off by hand early, or use insecticidal soap
- Vine weevil damage on container plants (grubs in compost)

---

### May
**What to do:**
- Plant out tender bedding and vegetable seedlings after last frost date (usually mid-May in most of UK)
- Harden off seedlings over 10–14 days before planting out
- Chelsea chop (late May): cut back asters, rudbeckias, sedums by half for bushier plants and later flowering
- Tie in climbing plants (roses, clematis, jasmine)
- Sow French and runner beans outdoors
- Deadhead spring bulbs but leave foliage until yellow (at least 6 weeks after flowering)

**Watch for:**
- Powdery mildew on roses if dry spell; improve air circulation
- Box blight on formal hedging
- Last frost risk in Scotland and upland areas

---

## Early Summer (June – July)

### June
**What to do:**
- Plant out tender perennials (dahlias, salvias, half-hardy fuchsias) — frost risk now past in all UK
- Summer-prune wisterias: cut new long shoots back to 5–6 leaves
- Sow biennial seeds (foxgloves, hollyhocks, wallflowers) for next year
- Divide and replant bearded irises after flowering
- Pinch out dahlias' growing tips for bushier plants
- Water regularly during dry spells — early morning or evening to reduce evaporation

**Watch for:**
- Lily beetle — bright red, remove adults and eggs (orange clusters under leaves) by hand
- Thrips on gladioli
- Capsid bugs causing distorted growth on many plants

---

### July
**What to do:**
- Deadhead roses, dahlias, sweet peas, and repeat-flowering perennials to extend season
- Prune early-flowering shrubs (weigela, philadelphus) immediately after flowering
- Summer-prune pleached trees and espalier fruit
- Take semi-ripe cuttings of shrubs (lavender, rosemary, viburnum)
- Sow spring cabbage and overwintering onions
- Feed heavy-flowering plants (dahlias, tomatoes) with high-potassium feed

**Watch for:**
- Powdery mildew during hot/dry periods
- Red spider mite on stressed plants under cover
- Vine weevil egg-laying on container plants

---

## Late Summer / Autumn (August – October)

### August
**What to do:**
- Plant ornamental grasses — they establish well in late summer warmth
- Take cuttings of tender perennials to overwinter inside (pelargoniums, salvias, fuchsias)
- Begin planting autumn bulbs: order and plant colchicums and autumn crocus
- Continue deadheading, but leave some seed heads of teasels, echinacea for birds
- Trim box and yew hedges — their last cut of the year

**Watch for:**
- Box blight — symptoms worsen in humid conditions
- Earwigs in dahlia flowers
- Leatherjackets in lawns — grubs of crane fly, present in soil

---

### September
**What to do:**
- Plant spring bulbs: daffodils, tulips, alliums, crocus. Tulips best planted in November.
- Lift and divide overgrown perennial clumps
- Plant new shrubs, trees, and hedging — excellent establishment window (warm soil, autumn rain)
- Collect and store seeds from annuals and perennials
- Raise lawn mowing height to avoid scalping as growth slows
- Pot up amaryllis bulbs for winter flowering indoors

**Watch for:**
- Vine weevil adults feeding at night on container plant leaves (notched leaf edges)
- Honey fungus — amber toadstools at base of trees, white mycelium under bark

---

### October
**What to do:**
- Plant bare-root trees, shrubs, and roses as soon as available (Oct–Mar)
- Lift and store tender bulbs: dahlias, gladioli, cannas, begonias — after first frost blackens foliage
- Plant tulip bulbs (October is ideal — reduces risk of tulip fire)
- Apply bark mulch 5–8cm deep around borders — after clearing debris but before ground freezes
- Reduce watering and stop feeding container plants
- Move tender container plants into shelter before frosts

**Watch for:**
- Honey fungus spreading from infected stumps
- Early frosts — protect tender plants with fleece

---

## Winter (November – February)

### November – December
**What to do:**
- Plant garlic, overwintering onion sets, and broad beans (for early crop)
- Prune fruit trees (apples, pears) during dormancy — December is ideal
- Check stored bulbs and tubers for rot every 3–4 weeks
- Plant containerised hedging: beech, hornbeam, hawthorn, yew
- Clean and oil garden tools before storing
- Cut back ornamental grasses and dead perennial stems — or leave for wildlife/winter interest

**Watch for:**
- Rabbit and deer damage on young trees — fit spiral guards
- Waterlogging on heavy clay soils; consider drainage improvements over winter

---

### January – February
**What to do:**
- Order seeds and bulbs early (popular varieties sell out)
- Sow sweet peas in deep modules — January indoors for early flowers
- Prune roses (H/T and floribunda) in February in SW England; March in North and Scotland
- Cut back ornamental grasses before new growth starts (late February)
- Prune buddleja hard back to 30–60cm (February)
- Apply a fresh mulch or compost to bare borders before spring growth

**Watch for:**
- Slugs and snails active in mild spells — treat proactively
- Late frosts in February can catch early-emerging bulbs — protect with fleece if sharp frost forecast

## Regional Timing Adjustments

All timings above are for Midlands / South England baseline.

| Adjustment | Change |
|-----------|--------|
| SW England / Cornwall | 2–3 weeks earlier for spring tasks; 3–4 weeks later for autumn storage |
| North England | 2 weeks later for spring planting out; 2 weeks earlier for autumn preparation |
| Scotland Lowlands | 3–4 weeks later spring; 3–4 weeks earlier autumn |
| Scotland Highlands | 5–6 weeks later spring; 5–6 weeks earlier autumn |
| Coastal (any region) | 2–3 weeks earlier than inland equivalent latitude |
| Frost hollow | Treat as 1 region colder for any planting-out decisions |
