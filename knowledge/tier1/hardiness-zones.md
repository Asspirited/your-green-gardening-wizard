# UK Hardiness Zones
# Source: RHS (Royal Horticultural Society) H1–H7 scale
# Use this file for location-based frost risk assessment and plant hardiness recommendations.

---

## RHS Hardiness Scale

The RHS uses H1–H7, not the US USDA system. Always reference UK hardiness ratings.

| Rating | Name | Min Temperature | Description |
|--------|------|----------------|-------------|
| H1a | Heated greenhouse | >15°C | Tropical — not outdoor hardy in UK |
| H1b | Heated greenhouse | 10–15°C | Sub-tropical — indoor or frost-free only |
| H1c | Unheated greenhouse | 5–10°C | Tender — outdoor summer only |
| H2 | Tender | 0–5°C | Half-hardy; needs protection Oct–Apr |
| H3 | Half-hardy | -5 to 0°C | Survives mild winters; risky in cold regions |
| H4 | Hardy in most places | -10 to -5°C | Hardy in most of UK except severe winters |
| H5 | Hardy in most of UK | -15 to -10°C | Hardy in all but the coldest regions |
| H6 | Hardy in all of UK | -20 to -15°C | Reliable everywhere including Scotland Highlands |
| H7 | Very hardy | below -20°C | Extremely hardy; no UK location challenges it |

**Practical rule:** Recommend H4+ for most UK gardens. H5+ for Scotland, high ground, exposed sites. H3 plants can work in SW England and coastal sites with shelter.

## UK Regions and Approximate Zones

### Mild / Coastal (Warmest — H3 plants often viable)
- Cornwall, Devon, West Wales coast
- Scilly Isles (almost H2 viable outdoors)
- Isle of Wight, Hampshire coast
- Dublin and SW Ireland coast
- Typical winter min: -2 to 0°C
- Plant tip: Myrtle, Chusan palm (Trachycarpus), Echium, Cordyline australis outdoors without much protection

### SW England and South Coast
- Somerset, Dorset, W Sussex, Kent coast
- Bristol (typically mild, rarely below -8°C)
- Typical winter min: -5 to -2°C
- Plant tip: Most H3+ plants hardy without extra care; Phormium reliable

### Midlands and East Anglia (Continental influence)
- Birmingham, Leicester, Norwich, Cambridge
- Colder winters with occasional sharp frosts
- Typical winter min: -10 to -5°C
- Plant tip: H4+ reliable; Buddleja, Roses (well-mulched), most hardy perennials fine

### London and the Home Counties
- Urban heat island effect — London city often 2–3°C warmer than surrounding countryside
- Typical winter min: -8 to -3°C
- Plant tip: H4 and most H3 plants with shelter; Banana (Musa basjoo) can overwinter with mulch

### North England — Yorkshire, Lancashire, Cumbria
- Variable: coastal Lancashire milder than inland moors
- Yorkshire Dales and Pennines can hit -15°C on exposed sites
- Typical winter min: -12 to -5°C
- Plant tip: H5 standard; H4 in sheltered lowland gardens; no tender exotics without protection

### Scotland — Lowlands (Edinburgh, Glasgow)
- Glasgow slightly milder due to Gulf Stream
- Edinburgh wind-exposed; cold but not extreme frost
- Typical winter min: -12 to -6°C
- Plant tip: H5+ reliable; H4 in sheltered spots; avoid H3

### Scotland — Highlands, Orkney, Shetland
- Harshest UK climate; frost risk year-round at altitude
- Coastal areas (Inverness, Ullapool) milder than inland glens
- Typical winter min: -20 to -10°C
- Plant tip: H6–H7 for reliability; H5 in sheltered coastal spots; wind hardiness often more important than frost hardiness

### Northern Ireland and Wales
- Similar to North England; coastal areas on W coast milder
- Snowdonia and Brecon Beacons — high-altitude equivalent of N England uplands
- Typical winter min: -10 to -5°C
- Plant tip: H5 standard; H4 in sheltered gardens

## Frost Dates (Approximate UK Averages)

| Region | Last spring frost | First autumn frost |
|--------|------------------|--------------------|
| SW England / South Coast | Mid-March | Late November |
| Midlands / London | Early April | Late October |
| North England | Late April | Early October |
| Scotland Lowlands | Early May | Late September |
| Scotland Highlands | Late May–June | August–September |

**Note:** These are averages. Climate variability means late frosts in May can hit most of England in exceptional years. Always advise hardening off tender plants until late May.

## Microclimate Adjustments

A garden's microclimate can shift its effective zone by 1–2 ratings:

**Warmer microclimates:**
- South-facing walls trap heat (adds ~2–3°C)
- Urban gardens (heat island effect)
- Gardens with a body of water nearby
- Sheltered from prevailing wind

**Colder microclimates:**
- Frost hollows (cold air drains into low-lying areas — can be 5°C colder than surroundings)
- North-facing slopes
- Exposed hilltops and coastal headlands
- Areas shaded by buildings or evergreen trees all winter

**Practical advice for users:** If they are in a frost hollow, advise going one hardiness level higher (e.g. choose H5 where H4 would normally apply). If they have a sheltered south-facing walled garden in Cornwall, H2–H3 plants can often overwinter.

## Common Queries

**"Is X plant hardy in my garden?"**
Ask for their region/postcode, then match against regional min temperatures above.
Always add: "In a sheltered spot" or "against a south-facing wall" qualifiers where H3 plants are on the margin.

**Mulching borderline plants:**
H3 plants in marginal zones can often be protected with a 10–15cm mulch of bark or straw over the root zone, applied in October. This raises the effective soil temperature by ~2–3°C.

**First winter failure:**
Many borderline plants fail in their first winter before they are fully established. Advise extra protection in year one even for nominally hardy plants.
