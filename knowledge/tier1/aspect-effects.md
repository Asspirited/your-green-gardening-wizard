# Aspect Effects — Sun, Shade and Orientation

## Purpose
How garden orientation affects available light, temperature, and moisture —
and which plants thrive in each condition. Used to ground recommendations
in real horticultural conditions rather than generic advice.

## South-facing — the premium aspect

**Conditions:** Full sun from mid-morning to late afternoon. Maximum solar
gain. Soil dries out faster than any other aspect. Frost pockets rare.
In UK terms: 6+ hours direct sun in summer.

**Temperature effect:** Wall-trained plants against a south-facing wall
can behave as if one RHS hardiness zone warmer — a useful trick for
marginally hardy plants like Ceanothus, Fremontodendron, Carpenteria.

**What thrives:**
Mediterranean plants, drought-tolerant perennials, sun-loving shrubs,
most vegetables (tomatoes, peppers, aubergines, squash, sweetcorn),
most fruit (apples, pears, plums, figs, peaches against wall),
roses (most varieties), lavender, rosemary, cistus, phlomis, salvia,
alliums, agapanthus, echinops, eryngium, verbena bonariensis.

**What struggles:**
Moisture-loving plants, woodland plants, hostas (will scorch),
astilbe, meconopsis, most ferns.

**Design notes:**
Plant tall species at the back/north end to prevent shading lower plants.
Gravel mulch reduces watering frequency and suits Mediterranean aesthetic.
Consider a water feature — drought-tolerant gardens look better with
the sound of water. Irrigation system worth investing in for productive gardens.

## North-facing — the challenge aspect

**Conditions:** Direct sun only in midsummer mornings and evenings.
Essentially shade for most of the year. Soil stays cooler and moister.
Frost lingers longer in spring. Walls stay cold.

**The opportunity:** Many excellent plants specifically evolved for shade.
A north-facing garden well-planted with shade specialists can be as beautiful
as any other aspect — the key is not trying to fight the conditions.

**What thrives:**
Woodland plants: Astrantia, Digitalis (foxglove), Aquilegia, Geranium phaeum,
Pulmonaria, Brunnera, Epimedium, Persicaria, Thalictrum.
Ferns: Polystichum setiferum, Dryopteris, Athyrium niponicum.
Hostas (but not in deep dense shade).
Hydrangea (especially H. anomala subsp. petiolaris for wall).
Camellia (prefers no early morning sun — north or west wall ideal; prevents
frost-damaged buds from thawing too quickly).
Euonymus, Mahonia, Sarcococca (excellent winter scent), Fatsia japonica.
Impatiens (busy lizzies) for summer colour.

**What struggles:**
Almost all vegetables (except salad leaves in partial shade),
most roses (need 4+ hours direct sun), lavender and Mediterranean plants,
most fruit.

**Design notes:**
Extend flowering season with sequence planting from early spring
(Pulmonaria, Hellebore) through summer (Astrantia, Geranium) to autumn
(Anemone x hybrida, late hostas). Light-coloured walls and pale materials
brighten the space. Mirrors used carefully can increase sense of light.

## East-facing — morning sun, afternoon shade

**Conditions:** Direct sun from sunrise until around noon or early afternoon.
Afternoon shade. Can be frost-prone — east-facing walls get cold morning
air hitting them after overnight frost. This is why Camellias do better
on west or north walls — east aspect thaws frozen buds too quickly.

**What thrives:**
Most shade-tolerant plants, plus many that actually prefer this aspect:
Camellias and Rhododendrons (if not frost-tender varieties) benefit from
afternoon shade. Aquilegia, Astrantia, Geraniums, many Roses (most tolerate
but prefer more sun — try shade-tolerant varieties like Rosa 'Darcey Bussell'
or R. 'Geoff Hamilton'). Hostas do well if slug-free. Clematis montana (loves
this aspect). Most vegetables need at least 4-6 hours sun — morning sun only
may be insufficient for fruiting crops but works for salads.

**Design notes:**
Morning light is cooler and cleaner — good for photographing the garden.
Excellent for sitting areas used in the afternoon (shaded by then).

## West-facing — afternoon sun, morning shade

**Conditions:** Shade until noon, direct sun from afternoon through evening.
Often the warmest wall in UK due to prevailing mild westerly winds from
the Atlantic. Evening sun means long, golden light in summer.
Good for tender wall plants and climbers.

**What thrives:**
Many tender climbers and wall shrubs do well:
Fremontodendron (California glory), Carpenteria californica, Ceanothus,
Coronilla valentina, Solanum laxum 'Album'. Roses particularly love
this aspect — afternoon/evening sun reduces black spot (morning dew dries
quickly). Wisteria excellent. Most fruiting plants, most perennials.
Evening-scented plants particularly rewarding here: Nicotiana, Hesperis
(sweet rocket), Lonicera (honeysuckle), Daphne.

**Design notes:**
West-facing terraces and seating areas catch evening sun — prime sitting
position on warm evenings. Scented planting is particularly rewarding here.

## Mixed aspect — partial sun, partial shade

**Conditions:** The majority of UK gardens — parts in full sun, parts in
shade. The key skill is reading the microclimate zones within the garden
and matching plants to each zone rather than treating the whole garden as one aspect.

**Zones to identify:**
1. Full sun zones (south/southwest corners, open beds)
2. Morning sun only (east-facing beds, east of large structures)
3. Afternoon sun only (west side of house, west of fences)
4. Dappled shade (under deciduous trees — good for woodland plants)
5. Deep dry shade (under evergreens, beneath overhangs — most challenging)
6. Moist shade (north side, below walls)

**What this means for recommendations:**
In a mixed garden, use sun-lovers for the sunny spots and shade-tolerants
for the shaded areas. The garden will actually support the greatest plant
diversity of any aspect. The danger is planting sun-lovers in shade and
shade-tolerants in full sun.

## Shade types — a useful distinction

Not all shade is equal. When asked for plant advice in shade, distinguish:

**Dappled shade** (under deciduous trees, pergolas with climbers):
Best shade type for plants. Morning or afternoon sun reaches the ground.
Most woodland plants thrive: bluebells, primroses, hellebores, geraniums,
Solomon's seal (Polygonatum), wild garlic, anemones.

**Partial shade** (4 hours sun or less, open shade):
Wide range of plants tolerate this. Most hostas, astrantia, aquilegia,
many ferns, most Hydrangea.

**Full shade** (less than 2 hours sun, typically under walls or dense canopy):
More challenging. Best plants: Fatsia japonica, Aucuba japonica (spotted
laurel), Sarcococca (sweet box — winter scent), Pachysandra terminalis,
Ivy, Epimedium, male ferns (Dryopteris filix-mas).

**Dry shade** (deepest under trees or overhangs — both shady AND dry):
The hardest combination. Reliable plants: Epimedium (most robust dry
shade plant in UK), Alchemilla mollis, Geranium macrorrhizum, Vinca minor
(invasive tendency — use with care), Lamium maculatum.
